Our tissues are populations of cells that acquire mutations, compete and are selected throughout life. In a small fraction of cases this Darwinian process culminates in cancer. We study its **dynamics** and its **drivers** across three complementary aims.

## Longitudinal, non-invasive tracking

Most of what we know about cancer evolution comes from single snapshots. We instead track evolution *over time*, using non-invasive samples — principally circulating tumour DNA (ctDNA) — so that clones can be followed as they rise, fall and respond to treatment. This reveals the tempo of evolution that a single biopsy cannot.

![Subclonal dynamics reconstructed from serial circulating tumour DNA samples, shown with cloneMaps and phylogenetic trees.](/images/research/ctdna-tracking.png)

*ctDNA reveals treatment-associated evolutionary dynamics after surgery, spanning monoclonal, polyclonal-monophyletic and polyclonal-polyphyletic patterns of relapse (TRACERx).*

## Somatic evolution & carcinogenesis

From detailed, multi-region snapshots of tissue biopsies we reconstruct how competing clones initiate, progress and metastasise. Distinguishing driver mutations from passengers — and quantifying the selection acting on them — is central to understanding how, and how early, high-risk evolution takes hold. This work spans oesophageal and lung cancer, including large collaborative cohorts such as TRACERx.

![Clonal dynamics reconstructed across serial samples of individual tumours.](/images/research/clonal-dynamics.png)

## Clinical biomarkers

Measurements of somatic evolution are only useful if they can be acted on. We develop clinical biomarkers to predict and detect high-risk evolution — turning evolutionary signal into tools for earlier detection, risk stratification and prevention.

## Methods & software

This work depends on new computational methods, which we build and share openly — from visualising clonal architecture (**cloneMap**) to tracking subclones from ctDNA (**ECLIPSE**) and detecting copy-number instability in low-purity samples (**AstroCNA**). See [Software](/software/) for the full set.
